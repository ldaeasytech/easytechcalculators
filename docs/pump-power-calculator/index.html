Why Do We Use Pumps?

Pumps are used to move fluids from one location to another by increasing their mechanical energy. They overcome elevation differences, friction losses, and system resistance in piping networks.

Theoretical vs Actual Pump Power

Theoretical pump power is the hydraulic power required to move the fluid:

Hydraulic Power = œÅ g Q H

Where œÅ is density, g is gravity, Q is flow rate, and H is total head.

Actual power requirement accounts for pump efficiency:

Actual Power = Hydraulic Power / Pump Efficiency

Because no pump is 100% efficient, actual power is always higher than theoretical power.

How Does Pipe Material Affect Flow?

Pipe material affects surface roughness, which influences the friction factor. Rougher materials increase friction loss and pumping power requirements.

Pipe Diameter and Schedule Number

Pipe diameter directly affects velocity and friction loss:

  • Smaller diameter ‚Üí higher velocity ‚Üí higher friction loss
  • Larger diameter ‚Üí lower velocity ‚Üí lower friction loss

Schedule number determines wall thickness. Higher schedule means thicker walls, smaller internal diameter, and potentially higher friction losses.
