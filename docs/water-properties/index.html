Scientific Basis of Calculations

Thermodynamic properties are based on internationally accepted formulations published by the International Association for the Properties of Water and Steam (IAPWS).

The primary reference is the IAPWS-95 formulation (Wagner & Pruss, 2002) for the thermodynamic properties of ordinary water substance.

Transport properties (viscosity and thermal conductivity) follow official IAPWS releases.

Saturated properties are obtained using numerical correlations derived from published reference data, while single-phase regions are evaluated using double interpolation.

Worked Examples – Water & Steam Properties Calculations

Below are solved engineering examples using the Water & Steam Properties Calculator. These examples demonstrate how to determine phase, thermodynamic properties, heat transfer, and turbine power using common input pairs such as T-P, P-s, and T-x.

1. Water Phase Determination (T–P Input Pair)

Problem: Determine the phase of water at 400 K and 1 MPa.

Solution:

First determine the saturation temperature at 1 MPa using the P-x mode (x = 0 or x = 1). The saturation temperature is:

Tsat = 452.99 K

  • If Tsat > T → Compressed (Subcooled) Liquid
  • If Tsat = T → Saturated State
  • If Tsat < T → Superheated Vapor

Since 400 K < 452.99 K, the state is: Compressed (Subcooled) Liquid.

2. Superheated Steam Properties (Power Generation Example)

Problem: Water is heated to 450 °C and 50 bar. Determine its thermodynamic properties.

Convert units first using our Engineering Unit Converter .

  • 450 °C = 723.15 K
  • 50 bar = 5 MPa

Using the T-P input pair:

  • State: Superheated Vapor
  • Density = 15.805 kg/m³
  • Specific Volume = 0.06335 m³/kg
  • Enthalpy = 3319.49 kJ/kg
  • Entropy = 6.8253 kJ/(kg·K)
  • Cp = 2.3800 kJ/(kg·K)
  • Cv = 1.7563 kJ/(kg·K)
  • Viscosity = 0.00002655 Pa·s
  • Thermal Conductivity = 0.06516 W/(m·K)

3. Boiler Feedwater Enthalpy & Heat Requirement

Problem: Feedwater consists of 85% condensate at 90 °C and 15% makeup water at 20 °C. Determine heat required to produce superheated steam at 400 °C and 40 bar.

Step 1 – Inlet Enthalpy

  • hcondensate (T-x, 90 °C, x=0) = 377.33 kJ/kg
  • hmakeup (T-P, 20 °C, 0.1 MPa) = 112.75 kJ/kg

hin = (0.85 × 377.33) + (0.15 × 112.75) = 337.64 kJ/kg

Step 2 – Outlet Enthalpy

At 400 °C and 4 MPa (T-P): hout = 3216.48 kJ/kg

Step 3 – Heat Required

Q = hout − hin = 3216.48 − 337.64 = 2878.83 kJ/kg

Therefore, 2878.83 kJ of heat is required per kg of steam.

4. Steam Latent Heat Calculation

Problem: Saturated steam at 350 °C flows at 2150 kg/h. Determine the heat transferred.

  • hg = 2565.24 kJ/kg
  • hf = 1672.76 kJ/kg

Q = ṁ(hf − hg)

Q = (2150 / 3600) × (1672.76 − 2565.24) = −533.01 kW

Heat released by steam = 533.01 kW.

5. Steam Turbine Analysis (P-h and P-s Method)

Problem: Steam enters turbine at 6 MPa, h = 3600 kJ/kg. Exit at 200 kPa, s = 6.8 kJ/kg·K. Mass flow rate = 10 kg/s.

(a) Exit Enthalpy

Using P-s input:

h₂ = 2576.92 kJ/kg

(b) Steam Quality

x = 0.9403 (94.03%) → Two-Phase Region

(c) Turbine Power

P = ṁ(h₁ − h₂)

P = 10(3600 − 2576.92) = 10,230.8 kW = 10.23 MW

Note: Actual turbine output will be lower due to mechanical and heat losses.

Water & Steam Basics: Thermodynamic States Explained

Water may exist in different thermodynamic states depending on temperature and pressure conditions. Understanding these regions is essential when using the Water & Steam Properties Calculator.

Subcooled (Compressed Liquid)

When water temperature is below its saturation temperature at a given pressure, it exists as a compressed liquid. In this region, properties depend on both temperature and pressure.

Saturated State (Liquid–Vapor Mixture)

At saturation conditions, liquid and vapor coexist. Only one independent variable (either temperature or pressure) is required because they are related by the saturation curve. Mixed states are defined using quality (x).

Superheated Vapor

When temperature exceeds the saturation temperature at a given pressure, water exists as superheated steam. Two independent properties are required to fully define the state.

Why Two Inputs Are Required

According to the Gibbs Phase Rule, a single-phase system requires two independent intensive properties to define its thermodynamic state. In saturated conditions, only one property is independent because temperature and pressure are interdependent.

What Are Steam Tables?

Steam tables list thermodynamic properties of water and steam at different temperatures and pressures. They are widely used in:

  • Power plant cycle analysis
  • Rankine cycle calculations
  • Boiler and turbine design
  • Heat exchanger design
  • Thermodynamics education
