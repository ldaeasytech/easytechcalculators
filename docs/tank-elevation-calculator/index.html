Why Do We Use Elevated Tanks?

Elevated tanks store fluid at a higher elevation to create pressure using gravity. The elevation generates static head, allowing fluid to flow without continuous pumping.

Advantages of elevated tanks:

  • Reduced energy consumption
  • Emergency water supply during power outages
  • Stable pressure distribution
  • Lower operating costs

Is Using an Elevated Tank Better Than Using Pumps?

It depends on the application. Pumps provide flexibility and high flow rates, but require continuous power and maintenance.

Elevated tanks:

  • Reduce long-term operating cost
  • Improve system reliability
  • Provide passive pressure generation

Many systems use both: pumps fill the tank, and gravity distributes the fluid.

Why Must Gravity Tanks Be Elevated?

Fluid flows from high pressure to low pressure. Elevating the tank increases static head:

Static Head = Elevation Difference

Without elevation difference, gravity-driven flow cannot occur.

What Determines the Required Tank Height?

Tank height must overcome:

  • Friction loss in pipes
  • Fitting losses
  • Entrance and exit losses
  • Required discharge pressure

Total required head:

Tank Elevation = Static Requirement + Total Losses

How Do Pipe Material and Diameter Affect Tank Elevation?

Rougher materials increase friction factor, requiring higher tank elevation. Smaller pipe diameters increase velocity and friction losses.

Larger diameters reduce losses but increase capital cost.

Can Tank Height Be Too High?

Yes. Excessive elevation may:

  • Increase structural cost
  • Create excessive downstream pressure
  • Require pressure reducing valves

What Happens If Tank Elevation is Too Low?

Flow rate will be lower than desired, and downstream pressure may be insufficient to overcome system resistance.
